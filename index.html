Drug label and package change: Drug Facts table

The Drug Facts table is a table included on the outer label or package of non-prescription drugs to help you easily find important information.

On this page

  • About the Drug Facts table
  • Using the Drug Facts table
  • Manufacturers’ requirements

About the Drug Facts table

The Drug Facts table shows important information on the label of non-prescription drugs, sometimes called over-the-counter medicines. It helps you make an informed decision when buying a drug product by allowing you to find and compare information quickly and easily.

Non-prescription drugs include all items with medicinal ingredients that you can easily purchase without a prescription. For example, these include everything from wart creams to pain relievers that you can find in a pharmacy or many other stores.

Some labels already contain a Drugs Facts table. Companies have until June 30, 2021 to fully switch over to the new label requirements. Therefore, you will gradually start to see Drug Facts tables on more non-prescription drugs as we near 2021.

The Drug Facts table is modelled on the Nutrition Facts table for food products and has easy-to-read information on how to use a drug properly and any possible warnings.

Natural health products

Manufacturers of natural health products like vitamins are encouraged to include a Product Facts table. This table is similar to the Drugs Facts table found on non-prescription drugs.

Using the Drug Facts table

The Drug Facts table is in the same order for all non-prescription medications to ensure that you can more easily:

  • find important information about the drug
  • compare different products

The simple format of the table will help you easily find:

  • active ingredients (medicinal), including:
    • the amount in each dosage unit, which can help avoid the risk of an accidental overdose by taking more than one product with the same active ingredient
  • inactive ingredients (non-medicinal), which are important to avoid potential allergic reactions
  • what the product is used for
  • warnings, including:
    • when the product should not be used
  • directions for use

  • paper insert within the packaging
  • website to access the information online

Further changes to the labelling of Non-prescription Drugs

Under the self-care framework, Health Canada is looking to update its approach to regulating self-care products such as cosmetics, natural health products and non-prescription drugs. Phase II of the framework will introduce, for consultation, targeted amendments to the Food and Drug Regulations to introduce a risk-based approach to regulatory oversight for non-prescription drugs. These changes are intended to align the oversight for non-prescription drugs with other self-care products of comparable level of risk. For low-risk, cosmetic-like products, a Drug Facts table will be voluntary. For information regarding the labelling of these low-risk, cosmetic-like products, see Labelling of Category I products Labelling of Low Risk Cosmetic-Like Products.

Manufacturers’ requirements

Manufacturers must have a Drug Facts table on the outer label of all new non-prescription drugs as of June 13, 2017. Existing, non-prescription drugs must display a Drug Facts table no later than June 30, 2021. Complete details are provided in the Good Label and Package Practices Guide for Non-prescription Drugs and Natural Health Products.

In order to create clear and effective product labels and packaging information, manufacturers must follow the Drug Facts table formatting and content specifications.

Application of Graduated Flexibilities

All non-prescription drugs, with the exception of low risk cosmetic-like products under the Self-care Framework, must apply the Drug Facts format. However, some products and package sizes may not have sufficient space to accommodate the standard format on the label.

Labelling of Low Risk Cosmetic-Like Products

Low risk cosmetic-like products include toothpastes, mouthwashes, secondary sunscreens, and acne washes as well as products for local use on unbroken skin such as: antiseptic skin cleansers, anti-dandruff shampoos, medicated skin-care products, and diaper rash creams. This information does not have to be presented in a Drug Facts table.
